Lean portfolio management
as outcome-based investment governance

A practical way to align investment, capacity and governance to measurable outcomes — without leading with frameworks.

Audience: Divisional COOs & portfolio leads (spine deck) • Appendices for CEOs / ExCo / CIOs

Context

The direction is clear. Execution needs faster steering.

Barclays is explicitly targeting a bank that is Simpler, Better and More balanced — with disciplined investment and measurable performance.

Simpler

  • Reduce complexity and technology debt.
  • Standardise and modernise processes.
  • Make operations more resilient.

Better

  • Improve customer outcomes across journeys.
  • Improve performance across businesses.
  • Drive efficiencies with digital and AI.

More balanced

  • Allocate capital to highest-returning areas.
  • Balance risk, returns and investment needs.
  • Increase predictability and confidence.

The problem

When everything is a priority, the portfolio slows down.

Common symptoms in large, regulated organisations: decisions are slow, work queues grow, and confidence drops — even when teams are capable.

Decision latency

Multiple committees. Late escalations. Long lead time to change course.

Hidden overload

Too much work in flight. The same critical teams are booked everywhere.

Project funding lock-in

Spend is committed to scope, not outcomes. Plans become constraints.

Status > evidence

RAG and milestones dominate. Outcome movement is hard to see early.

Key point: this is a portfolio system issue — not a team capability issue.
The idea

LPM is a decision system — not a delivery methodology.

It makes three leadership decisions explicit, repeatable and evidence-based.

What leaders get

  • Clear investment choices tied to outcomes.
  • Visible capacity allocation (trade-offs made explicit).
  • Governance based on evidence, not status.
  • Confidence to stop, pivot or double-down early.

What teams get

  • Fewer conflicting priorities and less churn.
  • Stable direction within clear guardrails.
  • Faster decisions on scope, sequencing and funding.
  • A shared definition of value and success.
How it works

Three decisions, repeated on a cadence.

The goal is speed with control: decide outcomes, allocate capacity, steer by evidence.

01

Decide outcomes

  • Agree a small set of outcome KPIs per portfolio.
  • Define “what success looks like” for 90 days.
  • Make trade-offs visible and explicit.
→
02

Allocate capacity

  • Allocate capacity by theme (e.g., growth / resilience).
  • Set guardrails (risk, spend, WIP, thresholds).
  • Fund value streams, not long scope commitments.
→
03

Steer by evidence

  • Review outcome movement and flow metrics.
  • Re-forecast and reallocate based on actuals.
  • Stop low-value work early; double down on winners.
What changes

From gates and commitments to guardrails and steering.

This keeps control, but shortens the loop between decision and outcome.

Typical today

  • Annual plan treated as a fixed commitment.
  • Funding tied to project scope and milestones.
  • Risk and compliance discovered late.
  • Governance focuses on status and conformance.
  • Stopping work is politically difficult.

With LPM

  • Plan sets direction; cadence sets steering.
  • Funding tied to outcomes and capacity guardrails.
  • Risk and controls integrated earlier and continuously.
  • Governance focuses on evidence and decisions.
  • Stop / pivot becomes a normal discipline.
Control with speed

Guardrails make faster decisions safe.

Guardrails replace repeated re-approval with clear boundaries for teams and leaders.

Capacity buckets

Allocate capacity across themes (e.g., resilience, growth, simplification).

Decision thresholds

Agree what needs escalation (spend, risk rating, dependencies).

WIP limits

Limit work in flight to increase flow and predictability.

Value evidence

Require outcome signals early (KPIs, customer feedback, risk metrics).

Wireframe note: you can add division-specific guardrails later (e.g., regulatory commitments, cyber thresholds).
Empiricism

Evidence replaces opinion in prioritisation.

A small, stable set of measures helps steer investment and explain trade-offs.

Customer

Outcome measures
e.g., NPS, complaints, digital engagement

Efficiency

Cost & productivity measures
e.g., processing time, unit cost, automation rate

Resilience

Risk & control measures
e.g., incident rate, remediation time, control health

Flow

Delivery system measures

Operating rhythm

A lightweight cadence keeps the portfolio aligned.

Shorter cycles reduce surprises and make reallocation routine.

Quarterly

Outcome & capacity reset

  • Confirm strategic themes and targets.
  • Adjust capacity buckets and guardrails.
  • Rebalance based on evidence.
→
Monthly

Portfolio steering

  • Review outcome movement and flow.
  • Resolve cross-team dependencies.
  • Stop / pivot decisions (fast).
→
Continuous

Value tracking

  • Update scorecard (actual vs target).
  • Re-forecast value based on actuals.
  • Communicate progress consistently.
How we start

Start small: a 90‑day pilot to prove the loop.

No re-org required. Stand up the decision cadence, guardrails and evidence — then scale.

Days 0–30

Align

  • Select one portfolio slice to pilot.
  • Agree outcome KPIs and targets.
  • Define initial guardrails and decision rights.
→
Days 31–60

Launch

  • Stand up monthly steering cadence.
  • Build a simple scorecard (actual vs target).
  • Make trade-offs explicit and visible.
→
Days 61–90

Steer

  • Run two steering cycles.
  • Make one stop / pivot decision.
  • Agree what to scale next.

Appendix A

Portfolio leads deep dive

How prioritisation, guardrails and steering work in practice (without turning it into a framework discussion).

A simple portfolio steering view (wireframe)

One page that enables decisions: value, flow, risk, and spend.

Inputs (evidence)

  • Outcome scorecard: actual vs target.
  • Flow: cycle time, predictability, WIP.
  • Risk: control health, incident trends.
  • Spend: burn vs guardrails.

Decisions (made monthly)

  • Stop / pause low-value items.
  • Pivot scope based on learning.
  • Reallocate capacity between themes.
  • Escalate only when thresholds are met.

Tip: keep the “prioritisation model” visible but lightweight: a small set of value levers (speed, prioritisation, efficiency, resilience) plus a clear definition of evidence.
