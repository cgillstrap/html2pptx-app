Lean portfolio management
as outcome-based investment governance

A practical way to align investment, capacity and governance to measurable outcomes — with faster steering and clear guardrails.

Context

The direction is clear. Execution needs faster steering.

Barclays is targeting a bank that is simpler, better and more balanced. That requires an investment system that can re-prioritise quickly as evidence changes.

Simpler

  • Reduce complexity and technology debt.
  • Standardise and modernise processes.
  • Strengthen resilience through simplification.

Better

  • Improve customer outcomes across journeys.
  • Increase operating effectiveness and efficiency.
  • Use digital and AI to reduce friction.

More balanced

  • Allocate capital to higher-returning areas.
  • Balance risk, returns and investment demand.
  • Increase predictability and confidence.
Message: LPM supports the strategy by improving investment steering.

3
The problem

When everything is a priority, the portfolio slows down.

These patterns show up in well-intentioned, regulated organisations. They are system issues, not team issues.

Decision latency

Approvals and escalations take weeks, not days.

Hidden overload

Too much work in flight across shared teams.

Funding lock-in

Spend follows scope commitments, not outcomes.

Status > evidence

RAG and milestones dominate; outcomes lag.

Message: The portfolio needs a faster decision loop.
4
The idea

LPM is a decision system — not a delivery methodology.

It helps you make faster, clearer choices about what to fund and how to govern progress.

What you gain

  • Investment choices tied to measurable outcomes.
  • Visible capacity allocation and clear trade-offs.
  • Governance based on evidence, not status.
  • Confidence to stop, pivot or double-down early.

What changes for teams

  • Fewer conflicting priorities and less churn.
  • Stable direction within clear guardrails.
  • Faster decisions on sequencing and scope.
  • A shared definition of value and success.
Message: Better governance, delivered more often.
5
How it works

Three decisions, repeated on a cadence.

The goal is speed with control: decide outcomes, allocate capacity, steer by evidence.

01

Decide outcomes

  • Pick 3–5 outcome KPIs for the portfolio.
  • Set targets for the next 90 days.
  • Make trade-offs explicit and visible.
→
02

Allocate capacity

  • Allocate capacity across themes (not projects).
  • Set guardrails: spend, risk, WIP, thresholds.
  • Fund value streams, not long scope commitments.
→
03

Steer by evidence

  • Review outcomes and flow monthly.
  • Re-forecast value based on actuals.
  • Stop low-value work early and reallocate.
Message: Less drama, more routine steering.
6
What changes

From gates and commitments to guardrails and steering.

You keep control, but shorten the loop between decisions and outcomes.

Typical today

  • Annual plans treated as fixed commitments.
  • Funding tied to project scope and milestones.
  • Risk and compliance discovered late.
  • Governance focuses on status and conformance.
  • Stopping work becomes politically difficult.

With LPM

  • Plans set direction; cadence sets steering.
  • Funding tied to outcomes and capacity guardrails.
  • Risk and controls integrated earlier and continuously.
  • Governance focuses on evidence and decisions.
  • Stop and pivot becomes a normal discipline.
Message: Faster decisions become safe decisions.

Control with speed

Guardrails make faster decisions safe.

Guardrails replace repeated re-approval with clear boundaries for teams and leaders.

Capacity buckets

Allocate capacity across themes you care about.

Decision thresholds

Define what needs escalation and what does not.

Work in progress

Limit WIP to increase flow and predictability.

Evidence rules

Require early outcome signals for key work.

Message: Guardrails replace friction with clarity.
8
Empiricism

One scorecard view enables better prioritisation.

A small set of measures keeps trade-offs objective and explainable.

Outcome measures

  • Customer: NPS, complaints, digital engagement.
  • Efficiency: unit cost, automation rate, cycle time.
  • Resilience: incidents, remediation time, control health.

Flow measures

  • Lead time and cycle time by work type.
  • Predictability: planned vs delivered.
  • WIP levels and dependency hotspots.

Use: steer investment based on trend, not anecdotes.

Message: Make value and flow visible together.

Operating rhythm

A lightweight cadence keeps your portfolio aligned.

Shorter cycles reduce surprises and make reallocation routine.

Quarterly

Reset direction

  • Confirm themes and outcome targets.
  • Adjust capacity buckets and guardrails.
  • Rebalance based on evidence.
→
Monthly

Steer decisions

  • Review outcomes and flow.
  • Resolve cross-team dependencies.
  • Stop and pivot decisions quickly.
→
Continuous

Track value

  • Update scorecard: actual vs target.
  • Re-forecast value using actuals.
  • Communicate progress consistently.
Message: Governance becomes shorter and more frequent.

Appendix A • steering

A simple steering view: evidence in, decisions out.

This becomes the monthly decision screen for the portfolio lead and COO.

Evidence (inputs)

  • Outcome scorecard: actual vs target and trend.
  • Flow: cycle time, predictability, WIP.
  • Risk: control health, incidents, tolerance breaches.
  • Spend: burn vs guardrail thresholds.

Decisions (outputs)

  • Stop or pause low-value work.
  • Pivot scope based on learning.
  • Reallocate capacity between themes.
  • Escalate only when thresholds are met.
Outcome: faster decisions with less re-approval.
14
Appendix A • prioritisation

Prioritisation becomes a transparent trade-off.

Use a small set of value levers and consistent evidence to compare options.

Value

  • Customer outcomes and growth impact.
  • Cost and productivity impact.
  • Resilience and risk reduction.

Confidence

  • Evidence strength, not narrative quality.
  • Dependencies and delivery risk.
  • Clear success measures and owners.

Cost of delay

  • Regulatory deadlines and tolerances.
  • Customer harm and service impact.
  • Opportunity windows and market timing.
Output: a ranked backlog plus explicit capacity allocation.
15
Appendix A • guardrails

A starter guardrail set you can agree quickly.

Define boundaries once, then let teams move fast inside them.

Portfolio guardrails

  • Capacity buckets (by theme) and limits.
  • Spend thresholds for escalation.
  • WIP limits and dependency rules.
  • Outcome evidence required by stage.

Risk and resilience guardrails

  • Controls and assurance integrated early.
  • Operational resilience tolerance triggers.
  • Cyber risk rating thresholds.
  • Data privacy and regulatory requirements.
Tip: start with 6–10 guardrails, not 30.
16
Appendix A • funding

Funding shifts from projects to capacity with guardrails.

You keep financial control while enabling faster steering.

Project funding mindset

  • Commit money to scope and milestones.
  • Re-approval needed to change course.
  • Value realised late and hard to measure.
  • Overload spreads across shared teams.

Capacity funding mindset

  • Allocate capacity to outcomes and themes.
  • Teams manage work inside guardrails.
  • Value tracked continuously via scorecard.
  • Reallocation becomes routine each quarter.
Mechanics (capitalisation, CARs) belong in a later pack.

Appendix C • positioning

Agile delivery improves teams. LPM improves decisions.

This is why LPM often unlocks benefits when “agile adoption” stalls.

  • Agile improves delivery throughput inside teams.
  • LPM improves prioritisation and funding across teams.
  • Together, they increase speed, control and confidence.
  • Without LPM, agile can become local optimisation.
Message: keep exec narrative outcome-first, then translate.
