The hidden costs

Three costs that compound as the pace of change accelerates

Delayed value

Capital is committed upfront based on projections. Long approval cycles and multi-year programmes mean value arrives late — and reality has often moved on by the time it does.

Diffuse prioritisation

When every initiative requires its own business case, the default is to approve everything that clears the threshold. The result: too many initiatives, none adequately resourced.

Late learning

Value is assessed at programme completion, not during delivery. By the time we discover something is not working, most of the budget has been spent — and the opportunity cost is invisible.

A different model

No fund manager would commit all capital to a single thesis and wait 18 months to review performance

Yet that is essentially how most organisations manage their technology investment portfolio. The alternative is not radical — it is disciplined portfolio management applied to change investment.

>
Continuous investment steering

Three shifts that change how investment creates value

A shared portfolio view

A single, transparent view of all technology investments — categorised by strategic outcome, not project name. Trade-off decisions become explicit. Leadership sees where every pound is placed and why.

Funding that follows value

Instead of committing all capital upfront, funding is released in stages tied to evidence of value delivery. Initiatives that deliver are accelerated. Those that do not are redirected early — freeing capacity for higher-value work.

Clear decision rights at every level

Delivery teams make day-to-day decisions within guardrails. Portfolio leads steer allocation at a regular cadence. Leadership sets strategic direction and reviews performance — not individual scope changes.

This is not about removing governance. It is about making governance faster, more evidence-based, and better connected to strategic outcomes.

How it works here

Investment steering at three levels

The model connects strategy to execution through a clear hierarchy of decisions — each level working at the cadence and granularity that fits its role.

Enterprise

Group ExCo and CIO

  • Set strategic themes and portfolio-level allocation
  • Review cross-divisional performance quarterly
  • Approve or redirect major investment shifts
→
Divisional

COOs and portfolio leads

  • Prioritise within divisional guardrails
  • Rebalance portfolio mix monthly or quarterly
  • Monitor value delivery against OKRs
→
Delivery

Product and platform teams

  • Execute backlog within funded capacity
  • Report value metrics continuously
  • Adapt approach based on evidence

A measured path

Prove the model, then scale it

This is a journey designed to build evidence and confidence at each stage — not a transformation programme with a distant payoff.

Phase 1 — Crawl

Pilot and learn

  • Select one portfolio slice for value management overlay
  • Define OKRs tied to strategic themes
  • Run alongside existing processes — no disruption
  • Expand recurring capital to cover technology lifecycle

Proves: Value-driven prioritisation improves outcomes without increasing risk

→
Phase 2 — Walk

Expand and integrate

  • Scale to additional portfolio slices
  • Pilot product-oriented teams with capacity-based funding
  • Stand up cross-divisional portfolio governance
  • Integrate value metrics into divisional reporting

Proves: The model works across investment types and divisional boundaries

→
Phase 3 — Run

Continuous steering

  • Portfolio steering is the default model
  • All investment managed through value streams
  • Enterprise governance sets direction and guardrails
  • Complex programmes use lean staged funding

Delivers: Faster value, better alignment, reduced waste, full visibility

Starting the journey

What the pilot looks like in practice

What stays the same

  • Existing approval processes for most projects continue unchanged
  • Financial controls, capitalization rules, and regulatory reporting are preserved
  • Divisional accountability and budget ownership remain with current holders
  • No new technology platforms required to start

Good pilot characteristics

  • A portfolio with some agile delivery already in place
  • A mix of "keep the lights on" and "change" work
  • A senior sponsor willing to experiment and review quarterly

What we add

  • A value management layer for one portfolio slice — OKRs tied to strategic themes, with quarterly review
  • A transparent portfolio view showing where investment is placed and what value is emerging
  • A lean business case format for one or two initiatives — hypothesis, MVP, evidence-based funding release
  • A structured comparison: same portfolio period, new model vs. legacy model outcomes

Timeline to first results

2–3 quarters to meaningful evidence on whether continuous steering improves prioritisation quality and time to value in the pilot portfolio
